Research

We study neural networks not as oracles, but as systems that can be audited.

Neural nets are black boxes that work. Our goal is to understand what they learn, why they generalize to unseen data, and how to know when we shouldn't trust them at deployment — especially in high-stakes domains like medicine.

Currently: medical imaging. A model that says "pneumonia" with 92% accuracy might be looking at a chest drain, not the lung opacity. We use Grad-CAM to visualize attention and ask: is this interpretable? Often, it's not.

Methods

CNNs from scratch (PyTorch), Grad-CAM, Score-CAM, Integrated Gradients. Datasets: NIH ChestX-ray14, ISIC skin lesions, synthetic shortcuts.

Evaluation

Not just accuracy. We track localization (IoU with clinical annotation), shortcut sensitivity, and human-agreement scores.

Output

6 research threads, weekly writeups, final showcase and open-source audit toolkit.
